Main Categories of Neurodiversity

Two main categories of neurodiversity that have the biggest impact on education and the school environment:

Learning

Social

You'll often hear people referring to a condition as a "learning disability/difference". A person with a learning disability has certain neurological differences, due to which they are unable to access the limited education provided to all students without specific accommodations and/or specially designed instruction.

Human relationships, particularly in group settings, are very complex. Neurological differences can further shape how individuals process information like social cues, facial expressions or body language. These differences also influence communication styles and a person's capacity for emotional regulation.

Special Education (SPED)

Special Education is the range of services provided to students with disabilities that would otherwise impair their ability to learn. It can take place in either a regular or separated classroom but is not a "one size fits all" program. Students who qualify for SPED have an IEP.

504 Plan (Named after Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act)

  • A legally binding document outlining accommodations that a student will receive in general education settings.
  • Always integrated.
  • Reviewed annually.

IEP (Individualized Education Program)

  • A legally binding document outlining the student's educational goals and special service they will receive to achieve those goals.

Accommodations

Students who have an IDD, are neurodiverse, or are part of the special education program will usually receive learning accommodations or modes to help them succeed. Since every student is different and has different skills or learning styles, accommodations for each student are customized to the individual. Here are some common learning accommodations:

  • Extended time on tests, and/or taking tests in quieter secluded areas
  • Visual, auditory, and/or written instructions (e.g. a graphic organizer + spoken and written down instructions for an assignment)
  • Noise cancelling headphones to mitigate distraction
  • Flexible schedules to learn when students are most attentive and focused, as well as supervised breaks
  • Sitting in specific areas in the classroom away from loud noises, crowds, and other distractions to mitigate sensory overload
  • Fidget toys for de-stress and focus
  • Lighting of the classroom can be adjusted to help students to mitigate sensory distractions
  • Instructional Assistants (school employees) can help students stay focused, understand content, and organize their schoolwork
  • Positive reinforcement as well as the repetition of tasks and policies to help students retain information better
  • Counselors may provide emotional or academic support

These are just some of the accommodations provided by teachers and counselors at different schools. It's important to remember not every neurodiverse student needs all these accommodations, and others may. Be respectful of students who may have a different learning style than yourself, and understand that it was something they were born with, not something they can control. In the end, we are all needing some help whether it comes to emotional support, tutoring, or physical support, but it differs from person to person.

Social

Human interactions often rely on unspoken signals like body language, facial expressions, and tone of voice. For some neurodivergent students, these cues can be challenging to interpret and respond to effectively.

Nonverbal Social Cues

Neurotypical

Neurotypical people often use nonverbal cues, figurative language, and can transition between topics smoothly.

Neurodiverse

Neurodiverse students may prefer direct communication and may interpret language literally. Eye contact can feel unnecessary or uncomfortable.

Sensory Overload

Bright lights, loud noises, or crowded environments can overwhelm neurodivergent students, making focus and comfort challenging.
